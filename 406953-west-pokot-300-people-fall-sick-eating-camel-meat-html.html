West Pokot: 300 People Fall Sick after Eating Camel Meat

- The camel is said to have been sick all day before dying in the evening when it was cut into pieces and shared among villagers

- Most of those affected were placed on a drip to stabilise their condition

- Majority of the patients had diarrhoea and vomiting, with some even complaining of body aches

Nearly 300 people have been admitted to various dispensaries in Pokot North, West Pokot county, after consuming contaminated meat from a dead camel.

The camel's owner, John Kokwang, said it was sick all day on Monday, March 22, before dying in the evening when it was cut into pieces.

Kokwang, his three wives and 19 children were among the victims who were still receiving treatment as of Thursday, March 25.

“The neighbours scrambled for the meat. On Tuesday, March 23, morning people who had eaten the meat woke up with diarrhoea,” Kokwang told The Standard.

Chepoisho Akopotum, one of his wives, said she cooked the meat and shared it with her kin while the whole village received a piece of meat, which some people took home to their families.

“I don’t know what kind of poison the camel ate. Currently, the situation is bad. We have a number of camels, but only one was sick," said Akopotum."The neighbours joined us to get a share of the meat. Some boiled the meat and others roasted it. Anyone who ate the meat was affected,” she added.

Medical staff from the Catholic health centres of Alale, Nasal, Amukuriat, and the Kacheliba Sub-county hospital have been dispatched to take care of the patients.

Most of those affected, according to Eunice Lorete, a nurse at Alale Dispensary, were placed on a drip to stabilise their condition.

She said the majority of the patients had diarrhoea and vomiting, with some even complaining of body aches.

“The whole village feasted on the carcass. Those who are badly affected are the elderly and children,” said Lorete.

According to Lorete, some people preferred to stay at home rather than seek care, but as their condition deteriorated, nurses were forced to go to the villages to help them.

Due to a lack of food in the region, residents ate the camel carcass without realising it was poisonous, according to Nauyapong assistant chief, Faustino Longora.

According to him, most of those who consumed the meat suffered stomach pains, fever and body aches.

Longora said that instead of going to the hospital, some victims turned to herbal medicine.

“We have picked some people and taken them to the hospital. Some fainted as we rushed them to the dispensary. Even children were affected,” he said.
